CORVUS LITE PRODUCT PLAN
Built sentence by sentence from the early company definition conversation


1. THE CORE IDEA

Corvus Lite is a lightweight operations control panel for small manufacturers, starting with machine shops.

It is not a cheaper MES.
It is not a miniature ERP.
It is not a full QMS.
It is not an AI system that pretends to be a machinist.

Corvus Lite is the operating layer for shops that still run on whiteboards, binders, spreadsheets, email inboxes, paper travelers, QuickBooks, tribal knowledge, and the owner's memory.

The central product thesis:

Corvus Lite helps small manufacturers know what is happening, what is blocked, what is late, what failed quality, and what needs to happen next, without forcing them to adopt a full MES, ERP, or QMS.

The product begins as a system of awareness before it becomes a system of record.

The first customer should feel:

"I still run my shop. Corvus keeps the jobs, paperwork, files, blockers, handoffs, and follow-ups from slipping."


2. WHO THIS IS FOR

The first beachhead customer is a 5 to 50 person machine shop.

This shop may have:

- CNC mills
- CNC lathes
- Manual machines
- A shop manager or owner who knows everything
- A few machinists and operators
- Paper travelers
- Jobs tracked in Excel or on a whiteboard
- Customer RFQs buried in email
- Drawing PDFs and CAD files scattered across folders
- Quality records in binders or spreadsheets
- No real MES
- No real QMS
- Maybe QuickBooks
- Maybe a shared drive
- Maybe a shipping station

The shop does not want a giant software implementation.
The shop does not want a consultant.
The shop does not want six weeks of setup.
The shop does not want a rigid enterprise system that makes people feed the software all day.

The shop wants fewer dropped balls, fewer late nights, fewer customer apologies, and less dependence on one exhausted person remembering everything.


3. THE POSITIONING

Possible positioning language:

"Know every job, blocker, file, and handoff without buying an MES."

"From paper travelers to production clarity in one afternoon."

"The simplest way for a small shop to track jobs, quality, and shipping in one place."

"A lightweight command center for machine shops that run on email, drawings, spreadsheets, and human judgment."

"Corvus Lite is a control panel and chat-native operations assistant for small manufacturers."

The important positioning boundary:

Corvus Lite should not be sold as "AI agents for manufacturing."

The customer does not care how many internal agents exist.
The customer cares whether jobs are clear, people know what to do, and nothing important gets lost.


4. THE PRODUCT FEELING

Corvus Lite should feel calm, practical, and shop-native.

It should not feel like enterprise software cosplay.
It should not feel like a dashboard built for investors.
It should not feel like a science project.

It should feel like:

- A daily control panel for the shop owner or manager
- A job board that actually reflects the floor
- A lightweight document and memory system
- A coordinator that asks staff simple questions through chat
- A place where RFQs, quotes, POs, drawings, travelers, quality records, and shipping readiness come together

The best first impression:

The user logs in and within 30 seconds understands:

- What jobs are active
- What jobs are at risk
- What jobs are blocked
- What jobs are waiting on material
- What jobs are waiting on inspection
- What jobs are ready to ship
- What needs attention today


5. THE PRIMARY USER-FACING OBJECTS

The user should not primarily see agents.

The user should see operational objects:

- Jobs
- Customers
- Files
- RFQs
- Quotes
- Purchase orders
- Tasks
- Issues / Bottlenecks
- People
- Quality signoffs
- Shipments
- Activity history

Internally, Corvus may use many agents. Externally, the product should look like one coherent operations system.

The main page could be called:

- Command Center
- Shop Board
- Control Panel
- Today
- Operations Desk

The strongest names are probably Command Center or Shop Board.


6. THE THREE-LAYER PRODUCT MODEL

Corvus Lite can be understood as three layers:

Layer 1: Control Panel

This is the visible application. It includes jobs, statuses, blockers, files, tasks, quality readiness, shipping readiness, and daily summaries.

Layer 2: Communication Layer

This is Slack, Teams, SMS, email, or another chat channel. Corvus asks staff simple questions, receives replies, and updates job records based on those replies.

Layer 3: Agent Layer

This is the invisible background execution layer. Specialized agents extract data, read drawings, compare documents, summarize activity, ask follow-up questions, and update structured records.

Most users should never need to know which agent did what.

An advanced/beta mode can expose an automation trace for debugging:

- Which agent processed the message
- What it extracted
- Confidence level
- What job record changed
- What field changed
- Why it asked a question

But this should not be the normal user experience.


7. THE TRUST BOUNDARY

Corvus Lite must not pretend to be a machinist.

This is a foundational design principle.

Corvus can own:

- Reading documents
- Extracting structured data
- Organizing files
- Creating records
- Comparing RFQs, quotes, POs, and drawings
- Flagging mismatches
- Flagging missing information
- Flagging obvious risks
- Asking staff simple follow-up questions
- Drafting quote language
- Drafting customer updates
- Preparing traveler drafts
- Tracking job state
- Tracking issues and bottlenecks
- Tracking signoffs
- Remembering past jobs

Humans own:

- Whether the shop can actually make the part
- Final quote approval
- Final price
- Setup time
- Cycle time
- Tooling decisions
- Work instructions
- Final committed delivery date
- Final traveler approval
- Quality signoff
- Shipping approval

The correct behavior is:

"I extracted the requirements, found three risk areas, checked this against your shop profile, found a similar job from last year, and prepared a quote draft. Please confirm setup time, cycle time, material cost, and committed delivery date."

The wrong behavior is:

"This part will take 4.2 hours and should cost $1,183."

Corvus should make human judgment easier to apply.
It should not replace human judgment where the system cannot be trusted.

Corvus should also avoid creating administrative overhead for the shop manager.

If a workflow asks for more detail than the shop naturally needs, it is probably too heavy for Corvus Lite.

The product should default to:

- Simple statuses
- Short checklists
- Chat-based updates
- Photos when helpful
- Human approval at important gates

It should avoid forcing:

- Detailed operation routing
- Excessive manual data entry
- Setup documentation unless the shop wants it
- Inventory precision the shop cannot realistically maintain


8. CUSTOMER JOURNEY FOR A MACHINE SHOP

The general flow of a machine shop job:

1. Customer sends RFQ or inquiry.
2. Shop reviews drawing, CAD, quantity, material, tolerances, special requirements, and requested due date.
3. Shop decides whether to quote.
4. Shop prepares and sends quote.
5. Customer accepts and sends PO.
6. Shop creates job.
7. Shop confirms due date.
8. Shop prepares material, files, traveler, and readiness checklist.
9. Job enters the queue.
10. Job moves into machining.
11. Job is paused, blocked, or completed.
12. Job goes to inspection if required.
13. Job may require outside processing.
14. Job becomes ready to ship.
15. Job ships.
16. The history becomes shop memory for future work.

A clean Corvus Lite lifecycle:

RFQ Intake
-> Drawing Review
-> Capability Check
-> Quote Draft
-> Human Quote Approval
-> PO Intake
-> Job Release
-> Build Readiness
-> Queued
-> In Progress
-> Machining Complete
-> Quality Review
-> Ready to Ship
-> Shipped

Optional branches:

- Blocked
- Outside Processing
- Rework
- Customer Clarification Needed


9. RFQ / JOB INTAKE

The first major workflow is RFQ intake.

Inputs may include:

- Customer email
- Drawing PDF
- CAD file if available
- STEP file
- DXF file
- Quantity
- Material requirement
- Requested due date
- Special notes
- Tolerances called out in email
- Attachments
- Prior customer context

The Job Intake function should turn messy inbound requests into a structured RFQ package.

The first "wow" moment should be:

"I forwarded three customer emails and uploaded two PDFs, and Corvus built my RFQ/job board."

The system should extract:

- Customer
- Contact
- Part number
- Part name if available
- Revision
- Quantity
- Material
- Requested delivery date
- Drawing files
- CAD files
- Special requirements
- Finish requirements
- Cert requirements
- Notes from customer email
- Missing information

Extraction should include confidence levels.

Example:

Material: 6061-T6 Aluminum [high confidence]
Quantity: 250 pcs [high confidence]
Finish: Black anodize [medium confidence]
Critical tolerance: +/- .0005 on bore diameter [medium confidence]
Requested delivery: June 14 [high confidence]
Missing: CAD file, inspection requirement, cert requirement

The Job Intake function should create clarity, not pricing.


10. DRAWING REVIEW

The drawing review function reads drawing PDFs and extracts manufacturing-relevant information.

This can be a separate background agent or a hidden part of intake.

It should extract:

- Part number
- Revision
- Material
- Units
- General tolerances
- Tight tolerances
- GD&T symbols
- Surface finish requirements
- Threads and tapped holes
- Critical dimensions
- Notes block
- Finish/coating/plating notes
- Heat treat notes
- Deburring/chamfer notes
- Inspection requirements
- Cert requirements
- Drawing scale
- Page count
- Revision history

It should flag:

- Very tight tolerances
- Complex geometry
- Deep pockets
- Thin walls
- Small radii
- Threading requirements
- Secondary operations
- Outside process requirements
- Missing dimensions
- Conflicting notes
- Old revision or unclear revision
- Quality requirements that may affect shipment

The drawing reader may eventually use RAG or a database of drawings, GD&T rules, and shop-specific examples.

For v1, the more practical target is:

"Here are the drawing notes that may affect quoting, production, inspection, or shipping."

Not:

"Here is a perfect manufacturability analysis."


11. CAPABILITY CHECK

The capability check compares the RFQ/drawing package against the shop profile.

The shop profile should come from a setup wizard during company onboarding.

This onboarding should feel practical, not like enterprise implementation paperwork.

The setup wizard asks the company for capability information in plain language, then Corvus structures it into a stored capability document used by the compatibility check.

The stored capability document may include:

- Machine types
- Machine work envelopes
- Materials commonly handled
- Materials avoided
- In-house processes
- Outside processes
- Inspection equipment
- Certifications
- Preferred job types
- Avoided job types
- Known vendors
- Minimum order size
- Standard lead time rules

The setup wizard can ask questions like:

- What machines do you run?
- What materials do you commonly machine?
- What materials do you avoid?
- What finishes or secondary processes do you do in-house?
- What outside processes do you commonly send out?
- What inspection equipment do you have?
- What certifications do you support?
- What job types do you prefer?
- What job types should Corvus flag as risky?
- What tolerances are routine, challenging, or outside normal comfort?

For the sake of being AI-native, the capability check should not only be a static rules lookup.

The structured capability document gives Corvus grounding.
AI can then evaluate the RFQ/drawing package against that grounding and explain the result in human language.

The capability check should combine:

- Structured shop capability data
- Drawing/RFQ extraction
- Known shop preferences
- Stored rules
- AI evaluation of ambiguous requirements

The capability check should flag obvious mismatches:

- Part too large for the shop's machines
- Required material is not supported
- Required process is not in-house
- Required certification is missing
- Tolerance is beyond normal stated capability
- Finish requires outside vendor
- Customer requested unrealistic delivery

The capability check should not attempt to identify every tool required.

Tooling decisions belong to the machinist.

The output should look like:

Capability check:
- Machining: likely compatible
- Material: supported, 6061 aluminum
- Finish: black anodize, outside vendor required
- Inspection: bore tolerance may require CMM or bore gauge confirmation
- Risk: requested delivery is aggressive based on current load

The user should be able to inspect why Corvus reached that conclusion:

- Which capability field was used
- Which drawing note or RFQ requirement triggered the check
- Whether the result came from a structured rule, AI interpretation, or both
- What the human should confirm before quoting


12. QUOTE PREP

The quote workflow should prepare a quote draft, not autonomously price the job.

Inputs:

- RFQ package
- Drawing review
- Capability check
- Similar historical jobs if available
- Shop quote template
- Human-provided estimates

Human-provided fields:

- Setup time
- Cycle time per part
- Programming time if needed
- Tooling estimate if needed
- User-entered material estimate
- Outside processing estimate
- Preferred margin
- Committed delivery date

The quote prep function can generate:

- Quote draft
- Assumptions
- Exclusions
- Lead time language
- Customer-facing notes
- Internal risk notes
- Missing inputs before quote can be sent

Good quote behavior:

"I can draft this quote, but need 4 human inputs:
1. Setup time
2. Cycle time per part
3. Material estimate
4. Confirm whether anodize is customer-required"

Then the machinist/shop manager fills those in.

The system can create a sample quote that the shop reviews before sending.

Corvus should not send quotes without approval.


13. MATERIAL COSTING

Material costing is useful but can wait.

In the UI, material costing can appear as:

Material Costing (coming soon)

For v1, this should be user-driven.

The machinist, shop manager, or estimator can enter the material estimate manually.
Corvus can store it, include it in the quote draft, and remember it for future reference.

In the future, a material costing function could:

- Estimate material required from stock dimensions
- Pull vendor pricing
- Compare available inventory
- Suggest order quantity
- Account for scrap/drop
- Identify cert requirements

For v1, material cost can be entered by the user.

Example:

Material cost: entered by user
Source: OnlineMetals quote / local supplier / internal inventory


14. PO INTAKE

Once a customer accepts a quote and sends a PO, Corvus creates or updates the production job.

Inputs:

- Customer PO
- Accepted quote
- Drawing/CAD package
- Confirmed quantity
- Confirmed due date
- Customer requirements
- Delivery address if present
- Shipping instructions if present

Outputs:

- Job number
- Job record
- Linked PO
- Linked quote
- Linked drawing package
- Confirmed quantity
- Confirmed due date
- Customer contact
- Delivery instructions
- Required docs
- Initial job status
- Traveler draft

The PO intake function should compare the PO against the quote.

This is a major value point.

It should flag:

- Quantity changed
- Price mismatch
- Due date changed
- Revision changed
- Material changed
- Added quality requirement
- Added cert requirement
- Shipping terms changed
- Customer referenced a different drawing

Example:

PO check:
- Quantity matches quote: 100 pcs
- Unit price matches quote: $18.40
- Due date changed: quote said July 12, PO requests July 5
- Drawing revision changed from Rev B to Rev C
- New note: Certificate of Conformance required


15. TRAVELER DRAFT

The traveler is the bridge between admin software and shop-floor software.

For v1, Corvus should create a traveler draft, not a full routing engine.

The PO intake workflow can create a basic traveler shell:

Job #: 1048
Customer: Acme Robotics
Part: Bracket-17
Revision: C
Quantity: 100
Material: 6061-T6
Due: July 12
Files: drawing.pdf, bracket.step
Required docs: C of C, final inspection
Outside process: black anodize

Corvus should not suggest detailed machining operations in v1.

The machinist already knows how to make the part.

The product should not create overhead by asking the shop manager to maintain operation-level paperwork unless the shop explicitly wants that later.

From Corvus's perspective in v1, the part is being made.
Corvus only needs enough state to understand whether the job is ready, active, blocked, complete, waiting inspection, ready to ship, or shipped.

Corvus should support printable travelers with QR codes.

The QR code can let a worker quickly open the job, update status, upload a photo, or answer a question.


16. PLANNING AND SCHEDULING

For v1, Corvus should not be a full scheduling engine.

Full production scheduling gets complicated very quickly.

Instead, frame this as:

Job Release + Shop Flow

From Job Intake to Job Start, every job should have a clear visual stage-by-stage flow.

This is part of the UI, not a heavy scheduling engine.

The flow should use arrows, stages, checkmarks, and a clear current-state highlight.
The user should be able to click a job and immediately see where it is, what stage is active, and what must happen before it can move forward.

The user should see a simple visual flow such as:

Released
-> Material Ready
-> Queued
-> In Progress
-> Paused / Blocked
-> Machining Complete
-> Inspection
-> Ready to Ship
-> Shipped

This flow can be represented as a lightweight flow chart or progress bar on the job page.

It gives the user visual clarity even if it is not a deep scheduling optimizer.

The flow should allow stage transitions:

- Mark stage complete
- Move to next stage
- Move back if something was wrong
- Open an issue if the stage is blocked
- Ask assigned person for an update through chat

The stage flow should reduce confusion, not create paperwork.
If a stage requires too much manual detail, the UI should default to a simple status and let the user add detail only when useful.

Useful planning fields:

- Confirmed due date
- Priority
- Assigned machine, optional
- Assigned owner/operator, optional
- Required material
- Required outside processing, optional
- Required inspection
- Required shipping docs
- Current status
- Blocker status

The product should answer:

- What jobs are due soon?
- What jobs are at risk?
- What jobs are blocked?
- What jobs are waiting on material?
- What jobs are waiting on inspection?
- What jobs are ready to ship?


17. ISSUES AND BOTTLENECKS

Bottlenecks should be treated like issues.

"Issue" may be the better product word because it is broader and more natural.

An issue can exist against a job, against a machine, or sitewide.

Bottlenecks are a type of issue.

This does not need to be a separate user-facing agent.

The system should support issue records:

- Machine down
- Waiting on material
- Waiting on customer clarification
- Waiting on tooling
- Waiting on programming
- Waiting on inspection
- Waiting on outside vendor
- Rework needed
- Staff unavailable
- Other

Each issue can have:

- Affected job, optional
- Affected machine, optional
- Severity
- Owner
- Opened date/time
- Notes/photos
- Status: open/resolved
- Source: manually created, chat-created, or Corvus-detected
- Related files/photos, optional

The background status watcher can say:

"3 jobs are blocked. 2 are waiting on material. Machine HAAS VF-2 is marked down and affects Job 1048."

Operators should be able to tell Corvus through chat that there is a problem.

Example:

Operator:
Job 1048 is stuck. VF-2 keeps alarming out.

Corvus:
Got it. Is this blocking production right now?

Operator:
Yes.

Corvus:
I created an issue for Job 1048 and Machine VF-2: "Machine alarm blocking production." I marked Job 1048 as Blocked and assigned the issue to the shop manager.

Corvus should ask for the minimum useful information:

- What job or machine is affected?
- Is production blocked?
- What is the problem?
- Who should own it?
- Any photo or note?

Issues should behave much like issue tracking in an MES, but without forcing MES-level overhead.


18. MATERIAL PREP AND BUILD READINESS

Inventory management is hard in a machine shop.

Raw material is consumed in chunks, drops, remnants, partial bars, sheets, and cutoffs.
Without an MES or disciplined inventory process, exact inventory can become untrustworthy.

For v1, material should be readiness-based, not quantity-ledger-based.

Instead of:

"You have exactly 41.3 inches of 6061 bar stock."

Start with:

"Material ready? Yes / No / Unknown."

The readiness checklist:

- Material required
- Material source
- Material status: unknown, needs order, ordered, received, available
- Enough for job: yes/no/unknown
- Certs required: yes/no
- Certs attached: yes/no
- Photo attached, optional

This keeps the human in control and avoids fake precision.

Later, an Inventory Agent can be added for shops that want tighter tracking.

But early Corvus Lite should not depend on exact inventory.


19. BUILD STATUS

No setup agent is needed.

The machinist knows what to do.

The system should track job movement:

- Queued
- Running
- Paused
- Blocked
- Machining complete
- Sent to inspection

"In setup" can exist as an optional status if a shop wants it, but it should not be required.

Corvus should not ask for setup details by default.

Updates should be quick:

- Shop manager moves a card
- Operator scans QR on traveler
- Operator taps status
- Operator adds note/photo
- Operator enters quantity completed/scrapped, optional
- Operator replies through Slack or chat

V1 production fields:

- Current operation, optional
- Machine, optional
- Operator, optional
- Quantity complete
- Quantity remaining
- Quantity scrap, optional
- Status
- Blocker reason
- Notes/photos

The background status watcher should monitor:

- Jobs due soon
- Jobs stuck too long in one status
- Jobs blocked
- Jobs missing material confirmation
- Jobs completed but not inspected
- Jobs ready to ship
- Staff who have not responded to update requests


20. QUALITY

Quality should remain lightweight but separate enough to protect trust.

The quality workflow activates when:

- Job is marked machining complete
- User says inspection is required
- FAI is required
- CoC is required
- Customer requirement mentions inspection or certs
- Drawing has quality notes

V1 quality workflow:

Machining complete
-> Corvus asks whether inspection is required
-> User selects: None, Final Inspection, FAI, CoC, Custom
-> Inspector reviews
-> Pass / Fail / Rework
-> Attach photos/docs
-> Inspector signoff

Inspection record can include:

- Inspector name
- Date/time
- Result: pass/fail/rework
- Notes
- Photos
- Measurements, optional
- FAI required: yes/no
- CoC required: yes/no
- Uploaded docs

No in-process inspection is needed in v1 unless the user adds it.

Corvus can help by saying:

"This drawing mentions final inspection and material cert. Do you want to require these before shipping?"


21. OUTSIDE PROCESSING

Outside processing is common but does not need its own v1 agent.

It should exist as a placeholder or branch in the job flow.

Common outside processes:

- Anodizing
- Heat treating
- Plating
- Painting
- Coating
- Grinding
- Welding
- Testing

Fields:

- Outside process needed: yes/no
- Process type
- Vendor
- Sent date
- Expected return date
- Returned date
- Docs attached

This can live inside the job record/build flow for now.


22. SHIPPING

The shipping workflow answers:

"Is this job actually ready to leave the building?"

Inputs:

- Job complete
- Inspection signoff
- Customer address
- PO shipping instructions
- Required docs
- Packing photos, optional
- CoC / FAI / certs if needed
- Carrier/tracking, optional

Shipping checklist:

- Quantity complete
- Inspection passed or waived
- Required docs attached
- Delivery address confirmed
- Customer PO attached
- Packing slip generated/uploaded
- Shipping photo attached
- Tracking number, optional

The "take a picture" idea is strong.

Photos create accountability:

- Packed parts
- Label
- Box/crate
- Shipping docs
- Final part condition

Corvus can draft:

- Packing note
- Customer shipment email
- Internal shipping summary

Example:

Ready to ship:
- Qty: 100/100 complete
- Inspection: passed
- CoC: attached
- Delivery address: confirmed
- Packing photo: attached

Suggested action: Draft customer shipment email.


23. CLOSEOUT AND MEMORY

No closeout agent is needed for v1.

But Corvus should quietly store closeout-style data:

- Actual ship date
- Whether job was late
- Blockers encountered
- Scrap or rework occurred
- Inspection failed
- Customer complaints
- Notes/photos
- Final docs

This becomes memory for future quotes, customer context, and risk detection.

The repeat relationship layer is not a separate workflow yet.
It is the database/memory getting richer over time.

Future Corvus can answer:

- What did we make for this customer before?
- What drawing revision did we use?
- What price did we quote?
- What issues happened?
- What setup notes helped?
- Did we ship late?
- Did we make money?
- Were there quality problems?


24. CHAT AS A REAL INTERFACE

For a small shop, the best UI may not always be the dashboard.

The dashboard is the control panel.
Slack, Teams, SMS, or email is the nervous system.

There should not be separate chats for different agents.

The user should experience one Corvus chat.

Behind the scenes, any internal agent should be able to request information through that shared communication layer, provide information through that layer, or ask a staff member a question.

The user should not need to know whether a question came from intake, quote prep, build status, quality, shipping, or another internal worker.

Normal user experience:

"Corvus asked John for an update on Job 1048."

Advanced/beta trace:

"Build Status Agent requested an update through the shared chat layer."

Corvus should be able to message staff:

Example:

Corvus:
John, quick check on Job 1048. Is it still running on VF-2?

John:
Yes. 72 done, should finish by 3.

Corvus:
Got it. I updated Job 1048 to 72/100 complete and estimated machining completion today at 3 PM.

Another example:

Corvus:
Maria, Job 1048 was marked machining complete. Does this require final inspection before shipping?

Maria:
Yes, final only. No FAI.

Corvus:
Done. I added final inspection as required and moved the job to Waiting Inspection.

The communication agent should ask tiny, answerable questions.

Bad:

"Please provide a full job update."

Good:

"Is Job 1048 still running, paused, or complete?"

Bad:

"Please update all production details."

Good:

"How many pieces are complete?"

Staff can use natural language:

"1048 is done, 98 good parts, 2 scrap from bad threads."

Corvus turns that into:

Job 1048:
- Status: Machining Complete
- Good qty: 98
- Scrap qty: 2
- Scrap reason: bad threads

Possible command patterns:

/job 1048 running
/job 1048 complete 100
/job 1048 blocked waiting on material
/job 1048 scrap 2 bad threads
/job 1048 photo

But natural language should be supported whenever possible.

The chat layer should also support issue creation.

Example:

"Job 1051 has a problem. Material is wrong."

Corvus should ask a short follow-up if needed, create an issue, attach it to the job, and update the job status if production is blocked.


25. JOB DETAIL PAGE

When the user selects an in-progress job, the job page should answer:

- What is this job?
- Where is it?
- Who owns it?
- What files are attached?
- What is blocking it?
- What has happened so far?
- What needs to happen next?

Example header:

Job 1048 - Acme Robotics - Bracket Rev C

Status: In Progress
Due: June 14
Owner: John Doe
Machine: Haas VF-2
Quantity: 72 / 100 complete
Current step: CNC milling
Risk: Medium

Possible tabs or sections:

- Overview
- Files
- Activity
- Checklist
- Quality
- Shipping
- Chat

The job page should include:

- Current status
- Stage-by-stage flow/progress visualization
- Attached drawing/CAD/PO/quote
- Extracted requirements
- Assigned people
- Quantity progress
- Issues / bottlenecks
- Quality requirements
- Shipping readiness
- Recent activity
- Suggested next action

The stage flow should be visually prominent.

For each job, the user should see something like:

RFQ Intake -> Drawing Review -> Capability Check -> Quote Draft -> Quote Approved -> PO Intake -> Job Released -> Build Readiness -> In Progress -> Quality Review -> Ready to Ship -> Shipped

The current stage should be highlighted.
Completed stages should be checked off.
Future stages should be visible but quiet.
Blocked stages should show an issue indicator.

The user should be able to:

- Move the job to the next stage
- Mark a stage complete
- Open or view issues attached to the current stage
- Ask an assigned person for an update
- See what information is missing before moving forward

This visual flow gives the job a clear narrative from intake to shipment.
It should feel like clarity, not bureaucracy.


26. ACTIVITY TIMELINE

Every job should have an activity timeline.

This makes invisible automation legible without forcing users to think in agents.

Example:

9:12 AM - John marked job as Running
10:04 AM - Photo uploaded from shop floor
11:30 AM - Corvus asked John for quantity completed
11:42 AM - John replied: 72 complete, 0 scrap
12:05 PM - Status updated to In Progress

Timeline events can include:

- File uploaded
- RFQ created
- Drawing reviewed
- Quote draft created
- Human approved quote
- PO received
- PO mismatch flagged
- Job released
- Material marked ready

28A. JOB FLOW UI

Every job should have a visual stage flow.

This is one of the most important UI ideas for Corvus Lite.

The flow should be accessible when a user clicks into any job.
It should show the full path from intake to shipment, with the current stage clearly marked.

The flow is not meant to be a detailed operation router.
It is meant to show the business and execution state of the job.

Recommended stages:

RFQ Intake
-> Drawing Review
-> Capability Check
-> Quote Draft
-> Quote Approved
-> PO Intake
-> Job Released
-> Build Readiness
-> In Progress
-> Quality Review
-> Ready to Ship
-> Shipped

Optional branches:

- Issue Opened
- Waiting on Customer
- Waiting on Material
- Outside Processing
- Rework

Each stage can display:

- Complete / current / future / blocked state
- Missing information
- Attached issue count
- Related files
- Assigned person
- Last update
- Next action

The flow should allow lightweight actions:

- Move forward
- Mark complete
- Open issue
- Ask for update
- Attach file/photo
- View history

This should not become operation-level paperwork.

Corvus should not require the user to define:

- Saw material
- CNC mill setup
- Tooling operation
- Deburr operation
- Detailed routing steps

Those details belong to the machinist and can remain outside Corvus unless a future customer explicitly wants routing.


28B. SETUP WIZARD UI

The company onboarding setup wizard should collect capability data.

The goal is to create a structured capability document without making onboarding feel like a consulting project.

The wizard should ask for:

- Company basics
- Shop type
- Machine list
- Work envelope limits
- Materials commonly supported
- Materials avoided
- Processes done in-house
- Common outside processes
- Inspection equipment
- Certifications
- Preferred work
- Work to avoid
- Normal tolerance comfort
- Challenging tolerance thresholds
- Standard quote/lead time preferences
- Key staff and roles
- Preferred communication channel

AI can help structure messy answers.

Example:

User writes:
"We mostly do aluminum and stainless, have two Haas mills, one lathe, no anodize in house, usually send that to ABC Coatings. We don't like titanium jobs."

Corvus structures:

- Materials supported: aluminum, stainless steel
- Materials avoided: titanium
- Machines: two Haas CNC mills, one CNC lathe
- In-house processes: machining
- Outside processes: anodizing through ABC Coatings

The user should be able to review and edit the structured capability profile before it is used.

This profile becomes the grounding document for capability checks.

33. THE COMPANY WE DEFINED TODAY

We spent the afternoon defining the company, not writing code.

The company is not just an AI wrapper.
It is not just a dashboard.
It is not a cheaper MES.

It is a new operational layer for small manufacturers.

The philosophy:

- Respect the machinist.
- Respect the shop manager.
- Respect messy reality.
- Do not force perfect data entry.
- Do not ask small businesses to behave like enterprise customers.
- Meet the shop where it already works: email, drawings, paper, chat, photos, and quick human judgment.
- Use AI to remove administrative drag, not to fake expertise.
- Make status visible.
- Make blockers obvious.
- Make handoffs clean.
- Make records searchable.
- Make staff updates easy.
- Make the owner feel less alone inside the chaos.

The best version of Corvus Lite is a calm, competent operations coordinator for small machine shops.

It watches the jobs.
It reads the documents.
It asks the staff simple questions.
It remembers what happened.
It tells the owner what needs attention.
It helps the shop move work from RFQ to shipped without losing the thread.

That is the company we started building today.
